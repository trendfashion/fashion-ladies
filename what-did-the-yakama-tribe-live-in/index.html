Answering the Target: What Did the Yakama Tribe Live In?

The Yakama Tribe lived in various types of dwellings depending on the season, location, and availability of resources. During the summer months, they lived in temporary structures called tule mat lodges, which were made from woven mats of tule reeds. These lodges were lightweight, easy to assemble, and could be taken apart and transported quickly.

In contrast, during the winter months, the Yakama Tribe lived in more permanent structures called pit houses. These were partially underground dwellings that were constructed with wood frames, bark or mats covering the walls, and earth mounds on the roof for insulation. The entrance was usually a tunnel that led down into the pit house, which helped keep the interior warm.

The Yakama Tribe also built sweat lodges, which were used for spiritual and physical purification ceremonies. These were small, dome-shaped structures made from willow branches covered with blankets or hides.

Tule Mat Lodges

When the Yakama Tribe moved to a new location during the summer months, they would construct tule mat lodges. When it was time to move to a new location, the tule mats could be easily rolled up, and the poles could be disassembled, making it easy to transport the lodge to a new location.

Pit Houses

Pit houses were the primary type of dwelling used by the Yakama Tribe during the winter months. These structures were partially underground and consisted of a circular or rectangular pit dug into the ground. The walls were constructed with wooden poles, and the roof was covered with bark, mats, or brush.

The entrance to a pit house was usually a tunnel that led down into the pit, which helped keep the interior warm. Inside the pit house, there was a central fire pit used for cooking and warmth. The smoke from the fire would rise to the roof and escape through a hole or smoke hole.

Sweat Lodges

Sweat lodges were small, dome-shaped structures used for spiritual and physical purification ceremonies. These structures were made from willow branches covered with blankets or hides. Inside, stones were heated in a fire and then placed in a pit in the center of the lodge. Water was poured over the hot stones, creating steam that filled the lodge.

Participants in the ceremony would sit in the lodge, praying and meditating in the steam. The ceremony was seen as a way to purify the body and spirit, and it was an essential part of Yakama Tribe's spiritual life.
